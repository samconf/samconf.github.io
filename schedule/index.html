11:15am-12:15pm Fashion, Food, and Feminism: South Asian Entrepreneurs –– Havemeyer Hall
Guests: Aashi Vel, Pooja Bavishi, Haroon Choudery, Tula Goenka
Description:

This panel celebrates the innovation of South Asian entrepreneurs. The founders of Malai, The Traveling Spoon, Revive, and Sakhi for South Asian Women will speak about their experiences about how they transformed their ideas into successful organizations and businesses. From a Brooklyn based ice cream company whose flavors include Lemon Cardamom and Sweet Corn Saffron to a non-profit dedicated to survivors of domestic violence, the diversity of organizations featured reflects the diversity of South Asian passion itself. The panel will follow with an opportunity to ask questions.

1:45pm-2:45pm A Fireside Chat on Confronting Hate –– Havemeyer Hall
Guests: Mubin Shaikh, Elisa Hategan
Description:

Mubin Shaikh, a former member of the Taliban, and Elisa Hategan, a former member of white supremacist group Heritage Front, come together to explore their paths to radicalization and deradicalization, and discuss the factors that push someone to join terror groups such as the KKK and Daesh (aka ISIS). They will also share their insight on how young people can a create resistance movement against hate.

Today, Shaikh works in counterterrorism and has testified before the UN Security Council, US Senate Committee on Homeland Security and Governmental Affairs, and NATO. He has also served as an undercover operative for the Canadian Security Intelligence Service. Hategan is a writer, speaker, freelance journalist and educator on far-right extremism. She has provided information on hate groups to police and testified against white supremacists in Federal Court.
3:00pm-4:00pm South-Asian Voices in the House of Hip-Hop –– Havemeyer Hall
Guests: Raj Halder, Lushlife; Rajuju Brown, Grumby; Svetha Rao, Raja Kumari
Description:

Hip-hop was borne out African Americans grappling with agency within America’s inequitable structure. Since, the house of hip-hop has built a dialect that helps artists deconstruct personal experience across borders and identities. This event will explore how South Asian identity comes to terms with the origins of hip hop, given anti-blackness in the subcontinent, while pushing the envelope forward for other marginalized voices. We are excited to engage Lushlife, Rajuju of Grumby, and Raja Kumari to talk about their journeys in the art through a panel discussion followed by an open-mic session. Audience members are encouraged to question and probe!
Sunday


11:00am-12:00pm Places of Partition –– Roone Arledge Cinema
Guests: Shahla Hussain, Haimanti Roy, Shumona Dasgupta
Description:

We will be exploring the immediate and long-lasting effects of Partition on places of current strife in Kashmir. Moreover, we will discussing Partition within the context of states and countries such as Punjab, Bengal and Bangladesh and how the Partition affected these locations primarily. Our panelists have studied in depth the effects of Partition and the current stresses and struggles it created on these prominent South Asian locations.
12:15pm-1:15pm Climate Change Doesn't Exist –– Roone Arledge Cinema
Guests: Naveeda Khan, other speakers pending confirmation
Description:

This panel hopes to explore and broaden discussion about a diverse set of topics—including climate adaptation tools, the potential for climate refugees, and environmental justice—in the context of climate change in Bangladesh and South Asia in general.

Professor Naveeda Khan will elaborate about the rural and riverine environments in Bangladesh and the crisis-inflected future of climate change, while other panelists will join this discourse to offer critiques and deeper analyses about climate refugees, environmental justice, and possible plans of action to counter the detrimental effects of climate change in broader South Asia.
1:30pm-2:30pm Threats to Free Press –– Roone Arledge Cinema
Guests: Ravi Agrawal, Namrata Brar, Dhrumil Mehta, and Madiha Tahir
Description:

This panel will focus on recent threats to the safety and freedom of journalists in South Asia and how this compares to the environment faced by journalists in the United States (in terms of increased political polarisation, legal action, and harassment from the President and others through social media). Beyond this it is also interesting to look at how each of these regions report on events in the other and how they shape rhetoric and are able to circumvent threats faced by local reporters.
